
# McAllen Mobile Park Event Calendar  
## Project Proposal and Research

## Part 1: Problem Statement

Residents of McAllen Mobile Park and similar small communities lack a simple, centralized way to manage, publish, and access event information. Events are currently shared through Facebook posts, word of mouth, and informal notices. In practice, a resident may hear about an event days later, scroll through a Facebook group trying to find details, or miss it entirely because the information was buried or never seen.

These methods are inconsistent and unreliable, forcing users to search through posts or rely on memory, which leads to confusion and reduced participation. Many residents also struggle with search-based systems and may not know how to enter effective keywords.

The primary audience is residents of McAllen Mobile Park, with an average age of approximately 60. Many rely on smartphones rather than computers and have limited experience navigating complex platforms. Some do not have Facebook accounts or are unfamiliar with locating past posts. Additionally, many users experience reduced eyesight and mild cognitive challenges, making it difficult to navigate dense or cluttered interfaces.

This problem matters because poor access to information reduces engagement and weakens community connection. Residents may miss events, arrive at the wrong time, or choose not to attend due to uncertainty. Organizers must repeatedly answer questions or repost information, increasing workload. Lower attendance also reduces revenue for the park association, limiting resources for future events.

## Part 2: Existing Solutions

### Visit McAllen
Visit McAllen provides official city-wide event listings and serves as a centralized and credible source of information. However, it is designed for a broad audience and includes many irrelevant events. Users must browse multiple layers of content, and the interface is not optimized for older users.

### Eventbrite
Eventbrite offers strong search and filtering capabilities but presents a large volume of unrelated events. Users must actively search and filter, which is difficult for the target audience. The interface can be overwhelming and complex.

### Facebook Events
Facebook is currently used but requires an account and presents information in a feed where posts become buried. There is no structured calendar view, and formatting is inconsistent.

### Shared Limitation
All platforms are mobile-compatible but not optimized for accessibility. They rely on dense layouts and complex navigation, making them difficult for older users.

### Proposed Solution Comparison
The proposed solution focuses only on relevant community events and presents them in a simple, calendar-first interface. It removes unnecessary content, eliminates searching, and avoids account requirements.

---

## Part 3: Proposed Approach

The application will be developed as a mobile-first website prioritizing simplicity, readability, and ease of navigation. The interface will mimic a traditional analog calendar and event announcement system.

Users will land on a calendar view and select a date to see events, similar to checking a printed schedule. The system will consist of:
- Landing page (calendar)
- Submission page
- Event detail pages
- About page

Navigation will remain minimal and consistent. Event pages will function as digital flyers with large text and simple layout.

The site will be built using:
- HTML
- CSS
- JavaScript

Google Calendar will serve as the event data source, and Google Forms will be used for submissions. The site will be hosted on GitHub Pages.

Project 01 will include structure, navigation, and placeholder content. Project 02 will include full functionality and refined usability.

## Risks and Mitigation

- Technical complexity in integrating Google tools  
  Mitigation: Use simple features and avoid automation  

- Submission system too complex  
  Mitigation: Use structured Google Form  

- Integration failure  
  Mitigation: Use manual fallback  

- Limited familiarity with tools  
  Mitigation: Use tutorials and incremental testing  

- Mobile usability issues  
  Mitigation: Test early and prioritize readability  

- Scope creep  
  Mitigation: Limit to core functionality
